op. XXI · early tape splicing

Forfex

Music made by cutting tape. Before samplers, before software, a composer made electronic music with a razor blade. You recorded a sound to magnetic tape, then physically cut the tape and glued the pieces back in a new order — a splice. Play it faster and it rises in pitch; turn the piece around and it runs backwards; join its ends and it loops forever. This machine is that studio. It synthesizes its own raw sounds, then composes by cutting and splicing them — you set the school, the montage, the cut, and how worn the tape is; the machine edits the reel.

On this music · the razor and the splicing block

What you're hearing

A generative piece assembled the way the first electronic composers worked: not by playing an instrument but by editing tape. The machine synthesizes a small library of raw sounds — electronic tones, bands of noise, struck bodies, a breath, a voice — then cuts them into segments and splices those segments into a reel: some sped up or slowed down (which raises or lowers their pitch), some turned backwards, some joined end-to-end into loops. The finished reel is played back through a modelled tape transport, so you also hear the medium itself: the slow pitch-drift called wow, the faster flutter, the warm compression of magnetic saturation, and tape hiss. The loop you hear is the reel; the WAV you cut is exactly that reel.

Why cut tape at all

When magnetic tape reached studios after the war it did something no instrument could: it made sound into a physical object you could hold, cut, and rearrange. A recorded sound became a length of tape. You could shorten it, splice it to another, run it at a different speed, or reverse it — edits impossible with a live performer. In 1948, in Paris, the engineer Pierre Schaeffer built Étude aux chemins de fer out of recorded locomotives and coined musique concrète: music composed from concrete recorded sound rather than written notes. With Pierre Henry he made whole works this way. The tape splice — a diagonal razor cut across an aluminium block, the two ends butted and joined with a strip of splicing tape — was the pen.

Paris, and then Cologne

A rival school formed in Cologne. Where Paris recorded the world, the West German Radio studio (WDR) built its sounds from nothing — pure sine tones and filtered noise from oscillators and generators, printed to tape and spliced together tone by tone. Karlheinz Stockhausen's Studie I (1953) is made of sine-tone mixtures; Studie II (1954) of bands of noise. A single "note" might be five sine tones recorded separately and spliced into one chord. It was painstaking, mathematical, and entirely constructed. This machine's Köln setting works in that palette — sines and noise-bands, clean butt splices, pitches organised in orderly rows.

London, and the loop

At the BBC's Radiophonic Workshop, Delia Derbyshire realised the Doctor Who theme (1963) with no synthesizer at all: each note of the melody was a single recorded sound, cut to length and its tape speed changed to reach each pitch, then the pieces spliced in sequence — a hand-built tune. And once you join a tape's two ends you have a loop: a phrase that repeats forever. In New York, Steve Reich (It's Gonna Rain, 1965) and Terry Riley ran two copies of the same loop at fractionally different speeds so they slid out of phase and slowly back again — music made purely from the drift between two tapes. The Radiophonique and Phase settings here are those two ideas.

Varispeed, and why the pitches are exact

Change a tape's speed and you change pitch and duration together — double the speed and everything jumps an octave and plays in half the time. This machine pitches its cut segments the same way, by varispeed, and it chooses only exact whole-number ratios — 3/2, 5/4, 4/3, and octaves of them — so every transposition is a pure just interval over a single reference of 220 Hz. Like the rest of this collection, there is no equal temperament in the notes. The wow and flutter you can dial up with Wear are the one deliberate detuning: the transport's unsteady hand, a modelled imperfection laid over exact pitches — not a tempered scale but a wobbling one.

Everything is synthesized — that is the point

Musique concrète famously recorded real trains and voices. This machine records nothing: every raw sound is generated in the page — additive sine mixtures, filtered-noise bands, inharmonic struck bodies, swept breath, a formant voice, a click. The character you remember from early tape music — the swoops, the reversed swells, the loops, the grain and hiss — comes not from the sources but from the process: the cut, the splice, the speed change, the worn transport. So the machine gives you the process, in full, and lets it act on sounds it made itself. That is faithful to how Cologne worked, and it lets a link or a cut WAV reproduce a pressing exactly, which no reel of real tape ever could.

The controls

Studio chooses the school and its palette and habits. Montage sets the density of the cutting — tenu holds long segments, haché chops fast. Cut chooses the splice: a franche butt cut edits hard and ticks at the join, a biaise diagonal cut crossfades smoothly, mixte mixes them. Wear ages the tape and the machine — from a pristine studio reel to a wobbling, hissing, saturated one. Pace sets the speed of the reel. The plate draws the reel flat, as an editor would lay it on the bench, every splice a razor line.

The name

op. XXI is named in Latin, the collection's tongue: forfex, the shears — the tool of the cut. The studios cut with a single-edged razor on a grooved metal block, but the art was always the cut itself: choosing where sound is severed and what it is joined to. Editing, made audible.
