On this music · change-ringing

What you're hearing

Not a tune. English tower bells are too heavy to play a melody in time — each swings full-circle on a wheel and can only be nudged a little on each pull. So ringers make music from order instead of pitch: they ring the bells over and over, altering which bell strikes when. Every distinct ordering is a “row”; moving from one row to the next is a “change”.

Ringing the changes

You begin in rounds — the bells in order from the lightest (the treble) down to the heaviest (the tenor), a descending scale. Each change may swap only neighbouring bells, and no row may repeat. Ring long enough under a good method and you thread a path through the permutations and arrive back at rounds: the ring “comes round”. To ring every one of the N! orderings once is to ring the extent; a full peal is 5,000 changes or more — around three hours, without a stop or a repeat.

Method and mathematics

A method is an algorithm, written in a shorthand called “place notation”; ringers memorise the path their own bell weaves through it — the “blue line” drawn on the plate above. It is applied group theory, and English ringers were generating permutations by adjacent swaps a century before mathematicians formalised the idea: Fabian Stedman's Tintinnalogia (1668) and Campanalogia (1677) are among the first books ever written on it. This machine rings five true methods on five to twelve bells — Doubles, Minor, Triples, Major, Caters, Royal, Cinques, Maximus. The Treble-Bob and Surprise methods (Cambridge and its cousins) and Stedman's own principle lie just beyond it.

Calls, touches and the conductor

A plain course is only one path home, and often a short one. To ring longer — and to steer the bells onto a chosen finish — a conductor calls out Bobs and Singles at the lead ends, small alterations that splice one course into another. A “touch” is any such calling that comes round; a full peal is a touch of five thousand changes or more. The one unbreakable rule is truth: a row may never be struck twice. Switch this machine to Touch (on Plain Bob) and it rings a genuine touch found by search — every one verified true, no row repeated — with each Bob (a dot) and Single (a diamond) marked on the plate as the conductor calls it.

The bells themselves

Each bell is synthesised from the partials of a real tuned bell: a hum an octave below, the prime, a tierce a minor third above it — which is why bells shimmer faintly minor even in a major ring — a quint, and the nominal an octave up, which fixes the “strike note” you think you hear. Tuning those partials true is called Simpson tuning, worked out in the 1890s.

The name

Change-ringing is an English art, so op. VII is named plainly in English: a peal is both the sound of bells and the word for a full-length performance.
