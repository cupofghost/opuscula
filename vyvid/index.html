On this music · vyvid

What you're hearing

Village singing from the southern steppe is polyphony without a score. One woman — the zaspivuvachka — opens each verse alone, the solo intonation called the zaspiv; the others catch the line mid-phrase and weave around her melody in close heterophony, each singing her own slightly different version of the same tune. One low voice anchors. And above the weave a single high voice — the вивід (vyvid, "the leading-out") — floats a descant that is less a harmony part than a second melody in the sky. Ethnomusicology calls the texture undervoice polyphony; the villages just call it singing together.

The tuning has no keyboard

No instrument sets the pitch — the ensemble tunes to itself. The unison is the anchor; fourths, fifths and octaves are pure; and the third is not a point but a zone, sitting between the piano's keys, which is why field transcribers mark it with little arrows. The intonation is social too: through a phrase four throats are four individuals, their unisons wide and gently beating — and at every line's cadence they converge and lock into one tone. This machine renders all of it exactly: pure ratios for the frame, a rational neutral third for the steppe manner, and a convergence law for the cadences. Nothing in it is equal-tempered.

The four voices

The voice itself is the open-throated chest voice sometimes called white voice — loud, straight, forward, made for open air, its brightness sitting in the harmonics rather than in breathiness. Vibrato, when it comes at all, arrives late and small on the long-held tones. Between lines the whole quartet breathes together; the silence is part of the song.

The steppe and the peninsula

Crimea's Slavic villages were settled from the late eighteenth century onward, from Ukrainian and Russian lands alike — often into the same village, the same street, the same family — and they sang from one shared repertoire pool in both languages: the drawn-out lyric protyazhna, the spring call vesnianka, the lullaby kolyskova. The style belongs to the broad southern-steppe continuum that runs through Tavria and Kherson to the Kuban. The peninsula's oldest musics — those of the Crimean Tatars — are another tradition entirely, with their own modes and masters, and are not attempted here.

The name

Ukrainian вивід — the leading-out — names the solo descant that crowns the texture. Op. XVIII is named for its highest voice, as the khöömei machine is named for the throat.
