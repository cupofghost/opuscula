On this music

Fado emerges from Lisbon's Alfama district in the 18th century, born from the longing of sailors' wives and the marginal communities of the port. It is a music of saudade — a melancholic yearning without cure — sung by fadistas (fado singers) accompanied by the classical 12-string guitarra.

The tradition rests on a small arsenal of harmonic changes: the "Fado progression" cycles through minor chords (typically Am-Em-Dm-E or variations), creating an emotional space where melody can move with freedom. Singers ornament these changes with melismas — long runs of notes on a single syllable — and slides between tones, each song a conversation between the voice and the guitar's responsive plucking.

This machine generates fado as a dialogue: the fadista melody is constrained to move within the mode but allowed to wander, while the guitarra's harmonic progression remains fixed, creating both structure and space for the voice's lament.
