On this music · the song of Lisbon

What you're hearing

A generated fado — the urban folk song of Lisbon. One synthesized voice, the fadista, sings a melody invented fresh each verse; under it the twelve-stringed guitarra portuguesa rings and the six-stringed viola (an ordinary acoustic guitar, as fado calls it) keeps the harmony and the beat. In the pauses between the singer's phrases the guitarra runs its baixaria — a bass counter-melody that answers the voice, the sound everyone knows even if they don't know its name. The whole piece is built from a small fixed frame; the machine fills that frame differently every time, so a link or a cut disc reproduces one exact performance.

Fado is a frame, not a tune

This is the part outsiders miss. A fado, to a Lisbon singer, is usually not a song but a template: a fixed chord sequence and rhythmic skeleton, named and shared, over which countless different poems are sung. The oldest and most important are the Fado Menor (in a minor key — slow, dark, the pure lament), the Fado Corrido (major, quick, the vehicle for satire and news), and the Fado Mouraria (named for the old Moorish quarter, major and moderate). A fadista knows the frame by heart and pours a poem into it, bending the melody to the words. That is exactly the machine's law: you choose the frame, and it composes a singer's line to fit — the same relationship, mechanised. Change the frame and the harmony, tempo and feel all change with it.

The guitarra, and the baixaria

The guitarra portuguesa is a pear-shaped, twelve-string instrument — six courses of paired steel strings — with a bright, shivering, almost harpsichord-like ring and a fan of watch-key tuners that give it its unmistakable silhouette. It doesn't just accompany: it converses. While the voice sings, it shadows; when the voice rests at the end of a line, it fills the silence with the baixaria, a quick melodic run in the lower strings that carries the ear to the next phrase. Here that call-and-response is built into the composition — the singer sings a phrase, then falls silent while the guitarra answers, verse after verse.

Saudade

Fado grew in the 1800s in the poorest quarters of Lisbon — the Alfama, the Mouraria — among sailors, dockworkers and the women who waited for them. Its subject is saudade: a Portuguese word with no clean translation, a longing for someone or something absent, a grief that is also almost sweet. The music is built to carry it — the minor mode, the sighing descent at the end of each line, the held notes that swell and break with vibrato, the singer leaning into a note a hair sharp or flat for the ache of it. In 2011 UNESCO listed fado as intangible cultural heritage of humanity.

How the voice is made

There is no recording of anyone. The fadista is synthesized: a buzzing glottal tone — the sound of vocal folds — is passed through formants, the resonances of the throat and mouth that turn a buzz into a vowel, and those resonances move as the "words" change. The line slides between notes (portamento, fado's constant sigh), decorates long syllables with quick runs (melisma), leans on a neighbouring note before the true one (the appoggiatura), and lets held notes bloom into vibrato. How much of all this you hear is the Sentimento control — from contido, restrained, to rasgado, torn open.

Why the tuning is just

Like the rest of this collection the notes use just intonation — intervals built from small whole-number ratios (a fifth is exactly 3:2, a major third exactly 5:4), tuned to a single 220 Hz reference, never the compromise of the piano's equal temperament. A real fado trio has no keyboard; it is a tight ensemble tuning to itself by ear, and a singer bends freely toward pure intervals — so just intonation is truer to how it actually sounds than the tempered frets would suggest. The one Hz number in the whole machine is that 220; every other pitch is a whole-number ratio of it, folded by octaves into each instrument's range.

The controls

Fado chooses the frame — menor, corrido or mouraria — and with it the mode, the chords, the tempo and the feel. Tom sets the key, low, middle or high. Sentimento sets how much the singer ornaments and how busy the guitarra's answer is. Andamento is the tempo, and it can be moved while it plays — the next bar takes it up. Tocar begins; each verse is composed anew as it goes, so it never quite repeats. The name is Portuguese, this collection's rule of naming each machine in its music's own tongue.
