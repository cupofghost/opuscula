On this music

May 7, 1747: Frederick the Great hands the visiting Johann Sebastian Bach a long, chromatic theme at the fortepiano in Potsdam and asks for an improvised fugue. Bach obliges, then goes home to Leipzig and mails back the Musical Offering — two ricercars, a trio sonata, and ten canons on the King's theme, several of them riddle canons: notated as a single line plus a Latin motto, the second voice left for the performer to find. One bears the inscription Quaerendo invenietis — "seek and you shall find."

Two of the ten carry mottoes that wire structure to meaning: the augmentation canon — Notulis crescentibus crescat Fortuna Regis, "as the notes grow, may the King's fortune grow" — and the modulating canon, the canon per tonos, which rises a whole tone every time through and could in principle rise forever: Ascendenteque Modulatione ascendat Gloria Regis.

Douglas Hofstadter's Gödel, Escher, Bach (1979) takes that endless canon as the emblem of a strange loop — a hierarchy that, climbed level by level, returns you to where you started — and pairs it with Gödel's discovery that a formal system can encode statements about itself as numbers inside itself.

This machine does what the riddle asks. Your seed is spelled, in base twelve, into six notes of the royal theme — decode them and you have the seed back; the URL is audibly in the tune. A deterministic search then looks for canons — retrograde, mirror-inverted, augmented, endlessly modulating — that verify as contrapuntally true against the theme, exactly the way a 1747 performer had to find them. The finale rises through six real, differently-coloured Werckmeister III keys and, at the seam, folds itself down an octave and restarts the piece: one strange loop that contains its own beginning.

Honesty: the search is sometimes allowed to bend a rule (marked licentia on the score) when no clean solution turns up in time; the octave fold at the seam is a real, exact −1200¢ drop, not an illusion; the B–A–C–H utterance that sounds across the fold is deliberately not derived by any rule in the piece — the one true statement the system cannot prove; and Werckmeister III (Bach's world, tuned at A=415, a semitone under modern pitch) is one plausible period tuning, not a documented certainty for this piece.
