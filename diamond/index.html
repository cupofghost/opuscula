On this music · Partch's tonality diamond

What you're hearing

A machine walking a closed circuit through Harry Partch's tonality diamond — a lattice of just-intonation chords built from small whole-number ratios, arranged so that every "bright" chord and every "dark" chord share exactly one tone with every other. The journey visits each key exactly once, leaving each one through the tone it shares with the next, and returns home at the end. Six of Partch's own instruments perform it, synthesized from first principles.

Harry Partch

Partch (1901–1974) burned his early conservatory-trained scores in a potbelly stove in New Orleans in 1930 and started over from the ratios up. He spent Depression years riding freight trains and living among hoboes — Barstow, one of his best-known pieces, sets hitchhiker graffiti he copied from a California highway railing to speech-melody. Because no existing instrument could play the intervals he wanted, he built forty-some of his own — some from artillery-shell casings, wine-glass tops, torpedo shells — and toured with the ensemble that played them. His aesthetic he called corporeal: music felt in the body, spoken as much as sung, against what he saw as the abstraction of the concert tradition.

A ratio is an identity

Ordinary chords are built by stacking; Partch built by ratio. Take the small odd numbers — 1, 3, 5, 7, 9, 11 — as identities, and every ratio of one identity over another is a tone. Read a set of them one way, stacked over a common denominator, and you get an otonality: a chord that is a literal slice of the harmonic series, the ancestor of the major chord — bright, blazing, outward. Read the same identities the other way, as a common numerator under different denominators, and you get a utonality: the harmonic series turned upside down, a chord of subharmonics — dark, hollow, inward, the ancestor of the minor chord that no tempered instrument can quite reach. Partch called the shimmer of moving between the two tonal flux.

The diamond

Partch's tonality diamond arranges every ratio of every identity over every other identity in a square grid; read down a column you get an otonality, read across a row its mirror utonality, and the pattern of ratios traces the shape of a diamond turned on its corner. In 1946 he built the Diamond Marimba, the whole diamond laid out as physical bars — one sweep of a mallet across a row or column rips out a complete chord. This machine's canvas shows that same lattice, rotated the same way, with the sounding key's row or column lit as the machine plays through it.

The pivot

Because every otonality and every utonality in the diamond cross in exactly one cell, there is always exactly one tone in common between any bright key and any dark key. Leaving a key through that shared tone — hearing it, then hearing it again as the first note of the next key — is the machine's only method of modulation. A Cloud-Chamber Bowl rings that pivot tone as each key closes, carrying it across the seam into the next. There is no other way the harmony moves.

392 Hz, and an honest limit

Partch's whole system, which he called Monophony, tunes everything from a single 1/1 — his was G at 392 Hz, and this machine keeps his choice. Every pitch here is exactly 392 times a small-number ratio, folded into an octave: there is no equal temperament anywhere in the signal path. Partch's full scale runs to 43 tones; the diamond itself supplies 29 of them at its deepest (the 11-limit). This machine honestly plays the diamond and nothing more — the other fourteen tones of Monophony are a possible future, not a claim made here.

Why it ends at home

The circuit always begins and ends on the otonality built over 1 — the plain harmonic series, the one chord nature tunes by itself without any human ratio-choosing at all. Every other key in the diamond is reached by leaving home and is eventually left again; the walk touches every key in the diamond exactly once before the final pivot lands back on 1/1. How long the machine lingers in the dark utonalities before that return, versus the bright otonalities, is the one control you have over the shape of the whole journey.

How the sound is made

No samples. The Marimba Eroica and Diamond Marimba are struck-bar models — a fundamental plus the inharmonic overtones of a real bar, plus a mallet transient. The Kithara and Harmonic Canon are plucked strings, Karplus–Strong synthesis (a filtered noise burst decaying around a feedback loop) — the Canon in a detuned double course, bright and short; the Kithara warmed and spread into a strum. Boo is bandpassed noise with a pitched chiff. The Cloud-Chamber Bowls are close-tuned partial pairs that beat slowly against each other, the way two real bowls never quite agree. The whole circuit is rendered once into a stereo buffer and looped with its long bowl-tails folded back across the seam, so the homecoming is seamless. The WAV you cut is exactly what you hear.

The name

op. XV is named in English — Partch's own tongue, and diamond is his own word for the instrument and the lattice alike.
