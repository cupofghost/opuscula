On this music · playground lore

What you're hearing

Almost the whole of it is three notes: a note, the note a minor third below, and the note a tone above — sol, mi, la. This is the teasing chant, the "nyah-nyah" figure, and it is very nearly universal: children arrive at it across languages and continents, and Leonard Bernstein opened his 1973 Norton lectures by proposing it as a musical constant of the species. Nobody teaches it. It is the first music transmitted entirely child-to-child.

The rhymes

The words of playground rhymes barely matter and are barely words. Their grammar is reduplication and ablaut — say a nonsense syllable, say it again with the vowel shifted: eeny, meeny; icka, bicka; hickory, dickory. This machine builds each rhyme fresh from that grammar, so every pressing chants verses that have never existed, yet all of them sound like something you half-remember from a yard somewhere. The rhythm follows the system Constantin Brăiloiu described in La rythmique enfantine (1956): strictly syllabic, built from short binary units, the same the world over regardless of the language spoken on top of it.

Counting-out

The counting-out rhyme is the playground's court of law: it decides who is It, and its verdict cannot be appealed. The chant travels round the ring, one syllable per child, and whoever the last syllable lands on is out; the ring closes and it begins again, until one child remains. It is an elimination process — a cousin of the Josephus problem, permutation mathematics performed by six-year-olds with total solemnity. Henry Carrington Bolton collected over eight hundred such rhymes in 1888 and traced some across a dozen countries. In this machine each elimination is real: that voice leaves the chorus and does not return.

The games

Clapping games and skip rope share one dramatic form: repetition under acceleration until the body fails. The verse comes round again a little faster, and again, and the game does not end so much as break — a fumbled clap, a foot in the rope — followed by the only correct response, which is laughter. The ring game is older and gentler: walk the circle, sing the round, and on the last line everybody falls down. This machine plays all of these honestly, mistakes included; the trip and the fumble are found by the seed, not by you.

The voices

Each child is synthesized as a small formant voice — two resonances shaped by the vowel being sung — and no two are tuned alike. A yard of children never sings in tune or in time, and must not: every voice here carries its own flat-or-sharpness and its own eagerness, arriving early or late by its own habit. Turn ROWDY off and they behave suspiciously well.

The lore, and the name

Iona and Peter Opie, who spent their lives collecting this material (The Lore and Language of Schoolchildren, 1959; The Singing Game, 1985), showed that playground rhymes travel faster than newspapers and persist for centuries, conserved by children who believe them to be brand new. The tradition is ancient: Horace, around 20 BC, quotes the children of Rome chanting rex eris si recte facies — "you'll be king if you play it right" — in their games, and the Latin word for such a jingle is nenia. Op. X takes that word: the small song, beneath literature, older than it.
