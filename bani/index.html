On this music · bani

Many-voicedness

Georgian traditional song is three-part vocal polyphony — mravalkhmianoba, "many-voicedness," proclaimed a UNESCO Masterpiece of Oral and Intangible Heritage. Everyone at the table sings the bass, the bani; a soloist, the mtkmeli ("the teller"), leads and states the text; a third voice, the modzakhili ("the caller-along"), answers above. The Kakhetian song "Chakrulo" rode the Voyager Golden Record into space in 1977 — it is, before anything else, table music, sung at the supra under a toastmaster's tamada.

Three corners, three textures

The regions realize the same three-voice spine very differently. Kakheti, in the east, spins out long unmetered drone dialogues — two soloists trading melismatic phrases over a bass that barely moves. Guria, in the west, is the famous contrapuntal trio: three genuinely independent lines, topped by krimanchuli, a rhythmic yodel that snaps between chest voice and falsetto. Svaneti, in the highlands, moves all three voices together through slow, archaic, dissonance-rich chords.

A tuning with no keyboard

No instrument ever stood behind this music, and the best modern measurements of it — Frank Scherbaum and Nana Mzhavanadze's analysis of Artem Erkomaishvili's 1966 self-overdubbed recordings, probably the earliest self-overdub session in ethnomusicology — show something stranger than a scale: fifths and fourths tuned near-pure, thirds sitting neutral between major and minor, and the tonal center itself drifting by measurable cents across a performance as the ensemble re-tunes to its own bass at every cadence. This machine renders that directly: every pitch is solved, not looked up, as a weighted negotiation between where a voice was, what it owes the chord, and a slowly drifting frame — locked hard to purity only at the line's end. Switch tsqoba to tempered and the negotiation stops: the drift reads zero, which is the point.

The name

Georgian ბანი (bani) is the bass line everyone at the table can sing — the floor the other two voices tune against. Op. XXVIII is named for it, the way khöömei is named for the throat.
