What you're hearing

A seeded dubstep dub, generated and synthesized in full — sub-bass, halfstep drums, dread atmosphere — dressed as a sound system stack at a dance. The machine composes the tune; you choose the session it plays and how much the bass is allowed to speak.

Croydon

Dubstep came out of one record shop. Around 2000, in the basement of Big Apple Records in Croydon, South London, producers like El-B and Horsepower Productions were stripping UK garage of its sweetness — darker chords, heavier subs, more space — and the shop's regulars included teenagers named Skream and Benga. The sound found its room at FWD>>, a night at Plastic People in Shoreditch whose system was tuned so low the music could live in frequencies most clubs never touch, and its broadcast tower in Rinse FM, a pirate station transmitting from tower-block roofs. On pirate radio the deep end had a name before the genre did: sublow.

The sound system

Underneath all of it is Jamaica. The sound system — a crew, a wall of custom speakers, and exclusive one-off acetates called dubplates — arrived in London with Caribbean migration, and with it came dub itself: King Tubby and Lee Perry remixing songs into skeletons of bass, drums and echo. Dubstep inherited the whole culture: the dubplate as the unit of exchange, the bassbins as the true instrument, the MC, and the rewind — when a tune lands hard enough, the selector wheels it back and drops it again. PULL UP does exactly that.

The halfstep law

The genre's law is austere. The tempo sits at 140 beats per minute, but the snare falls only on the third beat of the bar — halfstep — so the music moves at 70 while its hats shuffle at 140. That empty space is not a lack; it is the point. Into it goes the true melody-carrier: the sub-bass, felt more than heard, its line written low and slow. And when the bass moves above the sub it talks — a filter opened and closed by an oscillator locked to divisions of the tempo, quarter, eighth, triplet, sixteenth. The wobble is not vibrato; it is rhythm. This machine writes the bass pattern as a sentence of those divisions.

Meditate on bass weight

In 2005 Digital Mystikz — Mala and Coki, with Loefah and the MC Sgt Pokes — opened DMZ in a Brixton club, under the motto come meditate on bass weight. Their strain of the music is the deep one this machine wakes up in: sparse, patient, tribal percussion, a sine sub that swells rather than snarls, dub's cavernous space. The other sessions lean elsewhere — toward the dungeon, toward the mid-range chatter the later wobble era made famous, back toward the music's garage blood, and east toward the snake-charmer lines Skream loved.

Tuning

As elsewhere in this catalogue, everything voiced above the bass is tuned in just intonation — exact whole-number ratios from the sounding sub root, with the flat seventh taken as the harmonic 7/4 — so the pads lock beat-free onto the low end instead of shimmering against it. The sub itself is a near-pure sine, floored where the cabinets still speak, and nothing else is allowed below it: the kick is tuned short and tight so the sub owns the floor.

The name

Sublow — London pirate radio's word for the bottom octaves, and for this music in the years before "dubstep" settled. The machine speaks the scene's English throughout: you run a dub, you pull it up, you cut a plate.
