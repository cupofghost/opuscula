On this music · uilleann piping

What you're hearing

Generated Irish piping: a melody on the chanter over three steady drones, with keyed regulators vamping chords beneath — all tuned justly and synthesized from scratch.

The instrument

The uilleann pipes are Ireland's bellows-blown bagpipe, developed through the eighteenth century. Unlike the mouth-blown Highland pipes, the player pumps a bellows under one elbow — uilleann means “of the elbow” — feeding a bag under the other, which keeps the reed dry and lets the chanter sing sweetly across two octaves indoors. A full set adds three drones tuned to the tonic and three regulators, keyed pipes the piper strikes with the wrist to punch out chords. Alone among bagpipes, its chanter can be stopped dead against the knee, giving crisp, articulate phrasing.

Ornament as grammar

Because the pipes sound continuously and cannot be tongued, a piper separates repeated notes with grace-note ornaments — cuts, taps, rolls, and crans (the ornament used low on the chanter, where a roll won't fit). This machine treats that as law, expanding the ornaments as it generates reels, jigs, slip jigs, hornpipes and the free-flowing slow airs.

The name

Irish bolg — the bag, the belly of the pipes. The music is Irish, so the work is named in Irish.
