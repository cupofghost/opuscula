On this music · the modular synthesizer

What you're hearing

A modular synthesizer is a synthesizer taken apart. Instead of one instrument with fixed wiring, it is a rack of separate modules — a module that makes a tone, a module that shapes its loudness over time, a module that filters its brightness, a module that keeps time — and none of them are connected until you connect them, with patch cables, by hand. What you hear is subtractive synthesis: a raw, harmonically-rich waveform from an oscillator, carved down by a filter and gated by an envelope into a note.

Control voltage

The idea that makes it a system is that everything is the same kind of signal: a voltage. The pitch of an oscillator is a voltage; so is the brightness of a filter, the position of a note in a bar, the shape of an envelope. Because they are all voltages, any output can steer any input — an envelope can open a filter, a slow oscillator can wobble a pitch, a sequencer can play a melody by sending a staircase of voltages to an oscillator. A gate is the simplest of these: a voltage that is either on or off, the on-or-off that says now. Patching is composing: you decide what steers what.

The rack here

This one is patched for a techno loop, the way such systems usually are. A master clock divides into a sixteen-step sequencer; the sequencer's pitch voltage runs through a quantizer so it can only land on notes of the scale, then into an oscillator, a resonant low-pass filter and an amplifier — the classic voltage-controlled chain, VCO → VCF → VCA. A second oscillator takes its pitch from a sample-and-hold, which grabs a random voltage on each clock and holds it: a machine for melodies nobody wrote. A noise source and a pair of envelopes make the drums. A low-frequency oscillator drifts the filter open and shut. Everything meets in a mixer and runs out through delay and reverb.

The format

The modules are drawn in Eurorack, the small format defined by Dieter Doepfer in Germany in 1996, which turned modular synthesis from furniture into something you could carry and made an enormous cottage industry of module builders. The music itself descends from the Berlin School — Tangerine Dream, Klaus Schulze, Manuel Göttsching — who in the 1970s let sequenced modular patches run and evolve for the length of a side, and from the Detroit and Berlin techno that inherited the machines.

The name

Spannung is the German word for voltage — and, in the same breath, for tension. Op. XII is named for the thing the whole rack is made of: control voltage, Steuerspannung, one signal steering another until the system plays.
