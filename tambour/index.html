On this music · the French field drum

What you're hearing

The military drum is the oldest machine for moving a body of people in step. Before radio, before whistles, a regiment's day was run by sound: the tambour — the field drummer — and the clairon — the bugler — beat and blew a fixed vocabulary of signals that everyone in earshot had learned by heart. This is a French field battery playing those calls: a snare drum leads, a deeper field drum rolls under it, the bass drum marks the step, cymbals crash the accents, and a valveless bugle sounds over the top. Beneath it all a steel drone rings — the martial idea heard through its industrial descendants.

The step is the law

A march is built around walking. The French regulation step, the pas cadencé, is beaten at a fixed cadence — the ordinary march near 120 steps a minute — and the bass drum falls on the foot. Change the cadence here and the whole battery walks faster or slower; the drum does not play to the step, it is the step. That is the constraint this machine composes inside: the call fixes the figures, the cadence fixes the pace, and the snare embroiders within them.

The calls

Each named call is a batterie d'ordonnance — a regulation beating with a job. La Marche is the plain cadenced march. La Générale is the alarm that turns a whole camp out under arms — dense, rolling, urgent. La Charge drives the advance, fast and triplet-footed, the bugle screaming over it. Le Rappel recalls the troops; La Retraite is the dignified evening tattoo that ends the soldier's day. Aux Champs — "to the colours" — is the honours beating of ruffles (drum rolls) and flourishes (bugle fanfares) rendered to a person or a flag. Le Ban is the roll-and-stroke flourish that opens and closes a formal announcement — ouvrez le ban… fermez le ban. La Breloque dismisses the men to their own time, loosest of all.

The rudiments

French rudimental drumming is built from a small set of named strokes, and the snare here is written in them: the coup simple (a single tap), the fla (a flam — a grace note struck a hair before the main), the ra or drag (two grace notes crushed in ahead of the beat), and the roulement — the roll, a stream of bounced strokes that turns a series of hits into a sustained tone. The roll is the drum's held note; a march lives on the tension between the roll and the crack of an accent landing out of it.

The bugle

The clairon is a bugle with no valves and no slide. It can sound only the notes physically present in one tube's harmonic series — the bugler chooses a note by lip alone — so every call is written from the same four or five pitches. That physical limit is why bugle calls the world over share a family sound; the notes here are exactly those partials and nothing between them.

Martial, industrial

The Industrie control slides the battery from a clean parade ground toward a foundry. As it rises the drums are driven and saturated, a struck-steel anvil doubles the hard accents, and a low steel drone — the bourdon — swells underneath. This is the march heard through martial-industrial music, the late-twentieth-century genre that took military drumming, orchestral menace and machine noise and made the connection between the parade and the factory floor explicit.

A note on faithfulness

The regulation calls are a real, documented tradition, but they vary by era, corps and manuscript, and much lived in oral teaching. The rudiments and the shape of each call here are idiomatic — honest arrangements in the French field-drum style — rather than a claim to one authoritative score.

How the sound is made

No samples. The snare is a struck membrane plus a rattle of highpassed noise for the wires; the field and bass drums are membranes without the rattle; the cymbals are decaying metallic noise; the bugle is a brass tone whose upper partials bloom in as the note is blown, locked to the fundamental's harmonic series; the anvil is a cluster of inharmonic metal. The snare line is not one drummer but a rank — trebled, each copy nudged a hair in time and across the field, the way a massed corps sounds — with the field drum doubled beneath it. The whole phrase runs four bars, is rendered once to a buffer, driven through a shaper and a room, and looped — so the WAV you cut is exactly what you hear.

The name

op. XIII is named in French, the mother tongue of these calls: tambour is the drum, and in the old military usage the man who beats it — the boy at the head of the column whose two sticks set a thousand feet down together.
