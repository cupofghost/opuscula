On this music · the singing automaton

What you're hearing

In 2004 John Bergeron, a Los Angeles animatronics builder, made a music video for a machine he had built: Tara the Android, whom he called an attempt at "the world's first pop-star android." Tara is a pale mannequin who can move only her head and her hands. She sings a song called I Feel Fantastic — her words assembled by a crude speech synthesizer — while a cheap keyboard noodles behind her in a bright key that keeps not resolving. Uploaded to YouTube in 2009, the clip became a piece of internet folk-horror, less for anything it does than for what it is: relentless, mechanical cheerfulness with nobody home. This machine plays that straight — not the meme, the music: the automaton, the synthetic voice, the wandering accompaniment, and the small place where the cheer cracks.

The voice is really synthesized

Tara does not sing a recording; she is a speaking machine, and so is this. Every word here is built the way a formant synthesizer builds speech — the method behind DECtalk (the voice of Stephen Hawking) and the Speak & Spell. A buzzing source stands in for the vocal folds; a bank of three resonant filters — the formants — shapes that buzz into vowels, exactly as the mouth and throat do. The formant frequencies used here are the measured ones (Peterson & Barney, 1952; Klatt): ee is a low first formant and a very high second; ah opens the first formant wide; oo rounds both down. Consonants are the same physics — a burst of filtered noise for s and sh, a silence-then-pop for t and k, a low murmur for m and n. The words are spelled out in a small pronunciation dictionary, phoneme by phoneme, so the machine can only say what it has been taught to say.

Why she sounds uncanny

A human singer scoops onto a note, lets it waver, leans on the beat. Tara does none of this. Each pitch is set instantly and held dead flat — no glide, no vibrato, no give — because that is what a synthesizer does and what a person cannot. Masahiro Mori named the effect in 1970: the uncanny valley, the lurch we feel at something almost, but not quite, alive. The mechanical steadiness is not a bug to be smoothed away here; it is the instrument. The one deliberate softness is the mouth: the animated face opens and rounds with the vowel it is singing, driven by that vowel's own formants.

The synth that never lands

Behind the voice is an arpeggiator over a held pad — the auto-accompaniment of a home keyboard, bright and sugary. But its tonal center does not stay put: every bar it can slide by a step or two, so the chords wander through a loosely major space and functional harmony never quite closes. That drifting is what makes the backing sound, as one description of the original put it, faintly "atonal" — not twelve-tone rigor but a sweetness that has come untethered. The drift control is that leash: at zero the accompaniment loops in place; opened up, it walks away from the key while Tara sings on regardless, and the two fall out of agreement.

The affirmation, and the crack

The song's form is pure positive affect: I feel fantastic — hey, hey, hey, then the same words handed to you — you feel fantastic, you are fantastic — and a verse of small reassurances the machine generates fresh each time. Then, in the original and here, the cheer slips: Tara begins to sing run, run, run and please leave, in the identical bright deadpan, and the affect-and-content mismatch is the whole horror. The crack control decides how far in that unease reaches — from a pristine loop that never turns, to a routine so overrun that it stutters and breaks. Four routines reframe the same automaton: the canonical Fantastic, a too-warm Welcome that will not let you go, a chirping Jingle, and an open Malfunction. The words she reaches for are her own to a point — the seed writes the verse, the crack decides when it lands.

What moves, and what doesn't

The picture keeps the video's cruel economy: of the whole figure, only the head and the two hands move. The head turns and nods on a slow, seeded sway; the hands tick in small mechanical gestures with the beat; the mouth shapes the vowels. Everything else is held perfectly still. Turn on reduced motion and even that little stops — she simply stares out, mid-word.

The name

A persona was the mask a Roman actor wore, and the word was long read — folk-etymologically, but irresistibly — as per-sonare, "to sound through": the mask the voice comes through. It is also the root of person. Op. XXIX takes the word at both ends: a mask that sounds, and the thin, unsettling distance between that and a person. Everything is synthesized in the page; nothing is a sample; the same link always sings the same song.
