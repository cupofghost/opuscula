On this music

Across the Plains, a young man courted with a flute. The Lakota (Teton Sioux) call it the šiyótȟaŋka — the "big prairie-chicken flute" — its breathy, warbling voice likened to the booming courtship display of the grouse, and to birdsong. It was played alone, out on the prairie at dusk: a solo, unaccompanied love-song meant to carry across a camp. The tradition of Sioux music was documented in unusual depth by Frances Densmore, whose Teton Sioux Music (Bureau of American Ethnology, 1918) transcribed hundreds of songs.

The flute is a duct (fipple) flute: air is split against an edge, so the tone is soft, woody, and full of breath. Melodies favour a terraced descent — a phrase arcs up to a high note and tumbles back down in steps to a long-held tonic, the shared melodic shape of Plains song — ornamented with grace notes and quick "bird" trills between neighbouring tones.

On the tuning. A Plains flute is hand-made; no two are tuned alike, and older flutes were never in equal temperament — their finger-holes were spaced by hand, and their thirds often measure neutral, sitting between a minor and a major third. This machine renders the traditional minor-pentatonic fingering (the modern "Mode 1": root, minor third, fourth, fifth, minor seventh) in exact just intonation — the small-integer ratios a solo melody leans toward — over one chosen key. The Neutral third scale swaps the just 6/5 third for 11/9 (≈347¢), the flatter third measurements of old flutes often show; Major pentatonic is the brighter "Mode 4". The fingering diagram is a schematic — more holes open, higher pitch — not a literal chart of any one flute.
