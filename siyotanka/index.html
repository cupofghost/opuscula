The courting flute

Across the Northern Plains a young man courted with a flute. The Lakota call it the šiyótȟaŋka — šiyó, the prairie-chicken (a grouse), and tȟáŋka, big — its warbling, air-full voice likened to the bird's booming spring courtship display, and to birdsong generally. It is a duct flute: like a recorder, the player's breath is split against a sharp edge below a carved block (often shaped as a bird or animal), so the tone is soft, woody, and full of breath rather than clean. It was an intimate, solo instrument, played alone out on the prairie at dusk — a private song meant to carry to one listener across a camp, never part of the drum-and-voice music of ceremony.

Frances Densmore

The music of the Teton Sioux is documented in unusual depth thanks to Frances Densmore, whose Teton Sioux Music (Bureau of American Ethnology, Bulletin 61, 1918) transcribed and analysed hundreds of songs recorded on wax cylinders at Standing Rock. Her work — and the study of surviving old flutes — is why a machine like this can state a real tuning rather than guess one: the fingering maps to a pentatonic scale, and the intervals can be written as small whole-number ratios.

The terraced descent

Plains melody famously falls. A phrase tends to begin high and tumble downward in a series of steps and short plateaus to rest on a low, sustained tonic — ethnomusicologists call the shape a terraced or tumbling strain. That descent is the machine's melodic law: each verse is three to five free-rhythm phrases, each optionally climbing to a seeded peak a fifth to an octave-and-a-third above the root, then stepping back down — lingering now and then on an upper-neighbour oscillation — to a held tonic and a breath. How ornate the line is (how many graces and trills it sprinkles) is the Song control; how it breathes (seconds per note) is Pace.

On the tuning — and an honest limit

Every scale here is a pentatonic set given as exact just intonation: small-integer ratios over one chosen key, the pure intervals a solo melody with no accompaniment naturally leans toward. Minor pentatonic (the traditional "Mode 1" fingering) is root · 6/5 · 4/3 · 3/2 · 9/5. Neutral third swaps the just 6/5 third (316¢) for 11/9 (≈347¢), the flatter, "neither major nor minor" third that measurements of many old hand-made flutes actually show — a documented characteristic, not an invention. Major pentatonic is the brighter "Mode 4": root · 9/8 · 5/4 · 3/2 · 5/3. The one Hz number per key names the flute's lowest note; every degree above it is a whole-number ratio, folded by octaves, so nothing is equal-tempered. The honest hedge: a real Plains flute is hand-made, its finger-holes spaced by hand, and no two are alike. This machine renders the mode in exact ratios; the fingering diagram is a schematic — more holes open, higher pitch — not a chart of any single flute.

The controls

Scale chooses the tuning and the fingering; Key names the flute's root note (D, E, F♯, G, A). Pace sets how the flute breathes, and Song how much it ornaments — both can be moved while it plays, and the next verse takes them up. Play begins; each verse is improvised anew, so it never quite repeats, and the gap between verses is the player drawing breath. Press r (or the keys below) for another song — a fresh seed.

How the sound is made

One woody tone (a duct-flute harmonic profile as a PeriodicWave, through a brightness lowpass) carries the melody; under each note sits a breath-noise bed (bandpassed white noise gated to the note) and, at the onset, an attack chiff — the airstream striking the fipple. A gentle finger-vibrato blooms in after an onset delay, the way a held flute note warms. A faint prairie-wind bed (looping noise swept by a slow lowpass) breathes underneath, and the whole thing sounds into a seeded open-air reverb. A master limiter holds the peak.
