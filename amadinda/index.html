On this music · the amadinda and the court of the Kabaka

The court of the Kabaka

Buganda is the largest traditional kingdom of Uganda; its court at the Lubiri, the Kabaka's palace at Mengo, kept professional musician guilds for centuries — the twelve-key amadinda and the larger akadinda log xylophones, the entenga royal drum chime, the ennanga arched harp. In May 1966 government troops stormed the Lubiri; the court and its music stopped overnight.

How three play twelve keys

Two players sit facing each other across the middle keys. The omunazi plays okunaga ("to begin") — an even row of notes, always struck in parallel octaves — on the even pulses; the omwawuzi plays okwawula ("to divide"), the same kind of row, exactly in the gaps. Together they weave one unbroken stream at double density. A third player, the omukoonezi, composes nothing: he watches the combined stream and, on the instrument's two topmost keys, duplicates every note that lands on the two lowest keys — two octaves up. Nobody plays the melody you hear rising out of the texture; it is read off the whole.

Inherent patterns

At full speed — eight to ten strokes a second in real performance — the ear cannot track the true interlocking parts. It regroups the stream by pitch proximity into separate melodic threads that nobody is actually playing: what the ethnomusicologist Gerhard Kubik, who learned amadinda playing from the court musician Evaristo Muyinda, called inherent patterns. Kiganda composers judge a piece by what emerges, not by what is notated; Ligeti later named this music among the sparks for his late piano études.

The tuning

Klaus Wachsmann's measurements of Kiganda instruments in the 1940s–50s found five nearly equal steps to the octave, about 240 cents apart — an equipentatonic ideal, not a Western compromise. The proof is in the repertoire itself: a piece can be shifted by one to four steps (a muko) and remain exactly itself, which only works if every step truly is the same size. This machine tunes exactly to that ideal — five-tone equal temperament, no just intonation anywhere in the signal path — the collection's second deliberate departure from just tuning after TRITAVA's non-octave scale.

Muko — the same piece, five times over

Because the okukoonera rule always watches the instrument's two lowest physical keys — not two fixed notes of the tune — shifting the muko genuinely recomposes the derived part even though the melody underneath is, note for note, the same piece transposed. Five transpositions, five different emergent melodies.

What this machine does and does not do

It composes fresh okunaga and okwawula parts within the documented law on every seed — not a transcription of the historical repertoire, whose named classics (Ssematimba ne Kikwabanga, Olutalo olw'e Nsinsi, Ganga alula) survive through Wachsmann's recordings and Kubik's transcriptions and are honored here in name only. Two synthesized bodies carry the same score: the amadinda's dry hardwood slats, and the entenga drum chime that historically played the identical repertoire on a different body.
