What you're hearing

A seeded minimal-techno track, generated and synthesized in full — kick, bass, chords, hats and hiss — dressed as an unlabelled test pressing.

Detroit to Berlin

Techno was born in the mid-1980s in Detroit, in the drum-machines and synthesizers of Juan Atkins, Derrick May and Kevin Saunderson. Minimal techno pared it back to the barest hypnotic essentials. After the Wall fell in 1989 Berlin became its second home, and in the city's studios Basic Channel — Moritz von Oswald and Mark Ernestus — fused techno with the echo, reverb and cavernous space of Jamaican dub. The result trades melody for the slow, patient evolution of a single groove: the loop is the composition.

Affect and law

Seven Affekte — moods — set the key, tempo, palette, spread and density; a harmonic-narrative engine writes a long arc of longing and rare release (see below) while the arrangement writes itself. Six lean dark or industrial; the seventh, SOG, is the warm, rolling, stripped-back pocket of minimal deep tech — slower, sparser, more space between the hits. A “dubplate”, or white label, is the one-off acetate a DJ cuts to road-test a track before anyone else can get a copy.

Longing and release · the lamento ground

The harmony follows one of the oldest rule-books of grief in Western music: the lamento ground bass. From Monteverdi's Lamento della Ninfa to Purcell's Dido, a bass falling stepwise from tonic to dominant — 8 · ♭7 · ♭6 · 5 — was the codified emblem of longing, repeated endlessly while the voices above it varied. The descent never left; it is the harmonic skeleton of melancholic dance music to this day. Here every eight bars the bass makes that fall while the upper voices hold their grief — the mood's own deep minor-ninth colour, never a major chord — so the progression lives in the bass relation underneath: home, then falling, darker, darkest, open. Each chord is broken open into an arpeggio, one soft tone at a time, and the figure is varied every cycle — the old art of divisions — on a slow arc from sparse to full and back, the same patient build a dance record makes. Every tone above the bass is tuned in just intonation — exact whole-number ratios from the sounding bass (6/5, 5/4, 4/3, 3/2), not equal temperament — so the same figure is re-coloured at every step of the descent, and the upper voices lock beat-free onto the low end. Most cycles end unresolved: the ♭6 sighs onto the bare dominant with the minor ninth still hanging over it, and the descent simply begins again. Only rarely — where a mood grants it — is the dominant charged: the music strips to the bass and a single held interval, the suspension hangs at a pure 4/3 with the harmonic seventh's lean, resolves to 5/4 — the leading tone, tuned just over the dominant bass — and the next downbeat blooms home onto i before the ground resumes its fall. Some moods never grant it, hanging forever; one mood, IRON, takes the passus duriusculus — the "harder path", the chromatic descent Bach built the Crucifixus on — one semitone a bar, harsh by design.

Mixing in key

Turn on AUTO-SET and RILLE stops being one track and becomes a set: it beatmatches and blends into a fresh generated track every so often, the way a DJ threads a night together. It chooses the next key by the Camelot wheel, the harmonic-mixing system where adjacent numbers are a perfect fifth apart — so the move is a fifth up or down, the same key, or now and then a semitone/whole-tone energy lift. Each blend is a long, phrase-aligned crossfade with a bass swap: the incoming record's tops come up first while its low end is held back, then kick and bass are handed over cleanly at the bar so two basslines never fight. JETZT → triggers the next blend by hand. Every transition stays in key.

The name

German Rille — the groove cut into a record. Berlin is techno's adopted mother city, so the German is kept in view: the interface reads in English with the original German word beneath each label, and the code beneath keeps its German and Latin names.
