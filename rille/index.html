On this music · minimal techno

What you're hearing

A seeded minimal-techno track, generated and synthesized in full — kick, bass, chords, hats and hiss — dressed as an unlabelled test pressing.

Detroit to Berlin

Techno was born in the mid-1980s in Detroit, in the drum-machines and synthesizers of Juan Atkins, Derrick May and Kevin Saunderson. Minimal techno pared it back to the barest hypnotic essentials. After the Wall fell in 1989 Berlin became its second home, and in the city's studios Basic Channel — Moritz von Oswald and Mark Ernestus — fused techno with the echo, reverb and cavernous space of Jamaican dub. The result trades melody for the slow, patient evolution of a single groove: the loop is the composition.

Affect and law

Seven Affekte — moods — set the key, tempo, palette, spread and density; a harmonic-narrative engine writes a long arc of longing and rare release (see below) while the arrangement writes itself. Six lean dark or industrial; the seventh, SOG, is the warm, rolling, stripped-back pocket of minimal deep tech — slower, sparser, more space between the hits. A “dubplate”, or white label, is the one-off acetate a DJ cuts to road-test a track before anyone else can get a copy.

Longing and release

The harmony is written to ache, and it is not a loop — it is a sixty-four-bar story. Most of that story hovers: deep minor-ninth chords drifting among i, iv and the mood's darker neighbours, held for bars at a time, going nowhere on purpose — and never sounded whole. Each chord is broken open into an arpeggio, one soft tone at a time, always reaching for a shape it never states at once; each mood rolls its own way (rising, falling, rocking). And every tone above the bass is tuned in just intonation — exact whole-number ratios from the sounding bass root (6/5, 5/4, 4/3, 3/2), not equal temperament — so the upper voices lock beat-free onto the low end, the way the tuning works elsewhere in this catalogue. Major chords are rationed by law — when a ♭VI, ♭VII or Neapolitan ♭II passes through, it gets one bar and one quick upward glint, gone before it can turn bright. Resolution is rarer still: only where a phrase has charged up does the cadence come — the mode's own v is minor and cannot pull home, so its third is raised to a leading tone, and the music strips to its barest: the bass and a single held interval above it. The suspension hangs at a pure 4/3 with the harmonic seventh's lean, resolves to 5/4 — the leading tone of the key, tuned just over the dominant bass — and releases home onto 6/5 and 3/2 over i, as the bass walks down and home and the melody falls silent. Some moods never grant it: they end their phrases hanging on the dominant — a half-cadence, longing left open.

Mixing in key

Turn on AUTO-SET and RILLE stops being one track and becomes a set: it beatmatches and blends into a fresh generated track every so often, the way a DJ threads a night together. It chooses the next key by the Camelot wheel, the harmonic-mixing system where adjacent numbers are a perfect fifth apart — so the move is a fifth up or down, the same key, or now and then a semitone/whole-tone energy lift. Each blend is a long, phrase-aligned crossfade with a bass swap: the incoming record's tops come up first while its low end is held back, then kick and bass are handed over cleanly at the bar so two basslines never fight. JETZT → triggers the next blend by hand. Every transition stays in key.

The name

German Rille — the groove cut into a record. Berlin is techno's adopted mother city, so the work speaks German; the visible interface is translated throughout, while the code beneath keeps its original names.
