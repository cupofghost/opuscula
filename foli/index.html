On this music · the Mande drum ensemble

What you're hearing

West African drumming from the Mande world — the Malinké, Susu and Bamana peoples of Guinea, Mali, Senegal, Côte d'Ivoire and beyond. It is not a beat with fills on top; it is an ensemble of interlocking parts, each simple on its own, that combine into a single dense, cyclical rhythm. Every rhythm here has a name, and each name belongs to an occasion — a harvest, a dance, a rite of passage — with its own fixed parts that a whole village would know.

The instruments

The djembe is the goblet-shaped, rope-tuned hand drum, and it speaks in three voices: a deep bass from the centre of the skin, a ringing tone from the edge, and a cracking slap. Under it sit the dununs — three double-headed bass drums played with a stick, each with a small bell mounted on top that the same player strikes: the small high kenkeni, the middle sangban that carries the rhythm's signature phrase, and the great dundunba that booms underneath. Over everything rules the kariñan or kenken, a metal bell whose timeline is the clock the whole ensemble locks to. A gourd shaker strung with a net of beads — the sékéré — threads a dry rattle through the top.

How it holds together

No single part states the whole rhythm. The bell keeps an unchanging pattern; the kenkeni keeps the pulse; the sangban plays the phrase your ear learns to recognise; the dundunba drops the deep accents; the djembe accompaniments weave between them. The music lives in the gaps — one drum plays where another rests, so the composite runs faster and busier than any player. Mute a voice with the mixer above and you can hear the hole it leaves, and how the others still imply it.

The soloist

Above the fixed parts, one djembe leads — calling the ensemble in, signalling the dancers, and improvising. That lead is what this machine composes: seeded phrases, rolls, flams and accents, placed against the cycle, growing busier as you turn up the heat. The accompaniment is the law; the solo is free within it. In performance the lead also calls breaks — sharp signature phrases that turn the whole ensemble at once — the drummer conducting with the drum.

The rhythms

The named rhythms fall into two feels: binary (four-square, in 4/4) and ternary (a rolling 12/8, four beats each split in three). Kuku is a fast circle dance from the forest region; Kassa accompanies the harvest in the fields; Djansa and Sunu are celebration rhythms. In 12/8, Dundunba is "the dance of the strong men" from the Hamana; Tiriba and Mendiani are great Guinean dances, Mendiani for a chosen young dancer; Soli accompanies a rite of passage. The bell timeline of the 12/8 rhythms — a seven-stroke pattern — is one of the most widespread rhythmic figures on earth, shared across West and Central Africa and carried into the Americas.

Where it comes from — and a note on faithfulness

This is a living oral tradition: parts vary by village, teacher and lineage, and there is no single written score. The bell timelines here are the canonical ones; the drum parts are honest arrangements in the tradition's idiom rather than a claim to one true version — the same spirit in which the tradition itself is passed on, hand to hand. The djembe reached the world stage through Guinea's national ensembles — Les Ballets Africains, founded by Fodéba Keïta — and the teaching of master drummers (djembefola) like Mamady Keïta and Famoudou Konaté.

How the sound is made

No samples: every stroke is synthesized. The drums are struck membranes — a pitched tone with a fast downward pitch-drop and a stick or hand transient — the bells a cluster of inharmonic metallic partials. Bass, tone and slap are three different membrane strikes; muffled dunun strokes simply decay faster. The whole turning wheel is rendered to one buffer and looped, so the WAV you cut is exactly what you hear.

The name

op. IX is named in Maninka, the mother tongue of the music: foli is the playing itself — the rhythm, the act of drumming. "There is no movement without rhythm."
