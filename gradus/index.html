On this music · species counterpoint

What you're hearing

Two independent melodic lines, generated to obey the oldest rules of Western part-writing. A slow given melody — the cantus firmus — is laid down first, and a second voice is composed against it, note by note.

Fux and the five species

In 1725 the Austrian composer Johann Joseph Fux published Gradus ad Parnassum — “Steps to Parnassus” — a counterpoint treatise cast as a dialogue between a master (speaking for Palestrina) and his pupil. It teaches in five graded “species”: note-against-note; two notes against one; four against one; syncopated suspensions; and finally florid, free writing. The rules forbid parallel fifths and octaves, prize contrary motion, and prescribe exactly how a dissonance may be approached and left. Haydn, Mozart and Beethoven all studied from it — Beethoven's exercises survive — and it remains perhaps the most influential music-theory book ever written, distilling the pure vocal polyphony of the sixteenth century.

The rules are the instrument

Here those rules are switches. The machine writes by weighted-random search, backtracking whenever it breaks one; turn the rules off and the music changes character — lift the ban on parallel motion and it drifts toward medieval organum, the parallel singing that was Europe's first written harmony.

The name

Latin gradus, “a step”, straight from Fux's Latin title — the source language of the theory.
