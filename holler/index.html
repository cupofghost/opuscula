On this music · the old-time banjo

What you're hearing

An Appalachian fiddle tune played on the five-string banjo. Old-time tunes are short — two strains of eight bars, an A part and a B part, each played twice and then round again: A A B B, forever. The machine writes one such tune in the chosen mode and tuning; the control that matters most is the right hand, because on the banjo the picking pattern is as much the music as the notes are.

The fifth string

The banjo's oddity is the short fifth string, pegged halfway up the neck. It is a fixed high drone — you don't fret it, you just sound it — and it rings through everything, tying the tune to a single pitch the way a bagpipe's drone does. Nearly every style here uses the thumb to catch that drone on the offbeats; it is the sound your ear reads as "banjo".

Clawhammer, and the down-stroke

Clawhammer — also called frailing — is the old mountain hand, and it is played downward: the back of a fingernail strikes down onto a string for the melody note, the hand brushes down across the strings, and the thumb sounds the fifth-string drone. That figure — bum-dit-ty, a melody note then a brush-and-drone — is the engine of the style. Drop-thumb is clawhammer grown busy: the thumb "drops" off the fifth string to catch melody notes on the inner strings, filling every eighth and letting the tune move twice as fast.

The other hands

Before bluegrass there was two-finger up-picking — thumb leading the melody, one finger picking up on the offbeats — the lonesome older sound of the mountains. Then in the 1940s Earl Scruggs codified the three-finger roll: fast, syncopated arpeggios of thumb, index and middle, the melody note "picked out" of a continuous stream — the drive of bluegrass. Melodic (or "Keith") style, from the 1960s, spreads a scale across the strings so the notes ring into one another and fiddle runs can be played note-for-note. Same tune, five hands, five centuries of the instrument.

Modes and tunings

Old-time players don't think in keys so much as in tunings and modes. A banjo is retuned for the tune: Open G (gDGBD) for bright major tunes, Sawmill (gDGCD) for the haunting modal ones, Double C, Open D. The melodies live in the church modes — Ionian (plain major), Mixolydian (major with a lowered seventh, the "Old Joe Clark" sound), Dorian and Aeolian (the minor-tinged ones). That flat-seventh drone-against-melody is the whole flavour of the modal repertoire.

Where it came from

The banjo is an African American instrument. It descends from West African plucked lutes — the akonting, the ngoni — carried to the Americas by enslaved people, who built gourd-bodied banjos in the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries; the down-stroke, drone-string technique is African in origin. In the nineteenth century it passed into blackface minstrelsy and thence, on wax and in the hills, into Appalachian string-band music, where clawhammer and the old picking styles were kept alive. Every hand in this machine is a page of that history.

How the sound is made

No samples: every note is a plucked string modelled directly. A burst of noise is fed into a short delay line and cycled through a gentle filter — the Karplus–Strong algorithm — which is, quite literally, a mathematical plucked string: it decays bright and quick, exactly like a banjo. A little body resonance is added for the drum-head "pot", and the five right hands are just five different rules for when to pluck.

The name

op. VIII is named in Appalachian English, the mother tongue of the music: a holler is a hollow between the ridges — and the cry that carries down it.
