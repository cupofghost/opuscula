On this music · khöömei

What you're hearing

Every sustained note is already a chord. A voice (or a string, or a pipe) sounds not one pitch but a whole ladder of them at once — the harmonic series: the fundamental, then a tone an octave up, then a fifth above that, a fourth, a third, and on up in ever-closer steps, each an exact whole-number multiple of the first. We normally hear the stack fused into a single note with a timbre. The overtone singer pulls one rung of that ladder loose and makes it sing.

Two pitches from one throat

The trick is resonance. By narrowing the mouth and tongue into a tiny, sharply-tuned chamber, the singer builds a resonance so selective it amplifies just one harmonic of the droned fundamental and mutes its neighbours — a whistle floating a clear melody over an unbroken bass. Moving the resonance up and down the ladder plays the tune; because only harmonics of the drone can be selected, the scale is fixed by physics, not chosen. This machine does exactly that: a rich synthesized drone, and a razor-narrow filter swept across its harmonics.

The three manners

Khöömei proper is the middle, throaty voice, its overtones full and warm. Sygyt — "whistle" — squeezes the resonance until the overtone rings out piercing and pure, high on the ladder. Kargyraa drops the voice an octave into a growl: the vocal folds and the false folds above them beat together at half speed, so the drone doubles down into the chest and the overtones ride a deeper, rougher ground.

The steppe

Overtone singing belongs to the herding peoples of the Altai and the Mongolian west — Chandmani in Khovd is called its cradle — and to their Tuvan and Bashkir neighbours. It is bound up with a reverence for the sounding world: the wind over grass, water over stone, the cliff that answers back. Singers speak of imitating a landscape rather than composing against it. UNESCO inscribed Mongolian khöömei on its list of the Intangible Cultural Heritage of Humanity in 2010. The morin khuur — the horse-head fiddle whose two strings can bow a drone and a fifth beneath the voice — is its usual companion, and so is the tovshuur, the small two-stringed plucked lute of the Altai and Oirat, tuned in fourths and struck in a steady drive under the voice. Switch on either accompaniment and it joins in — the lute carrying on through the singer's breaths.

The name

Mongolian khöömei (хөөмий) means the pharynx, the throat itself — the instrument named for the part of the body that plays it. Op. XI is titled in that tongue.
