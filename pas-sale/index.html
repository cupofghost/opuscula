What you're hearing

A synthesized zydeco band — piano-accordion, frottoir (the corrugated metal rubboard worn like a breastplate and raked with bottle-openers), guitar, bass and drums — playing a two-step in G. Nothing was recorded; every voice is built from oscillators and noise in the page.

The music

Zydeco is the dance music of Black Creoles on the prairies of southwest Louisiana. It grew out of la-la, the old Creole house-dance music, and the accordion tradition captured on Amédé Ardoin's recordings of the 1930s. In the 1950s Clifton Chenier — the “King of Zydeco” — married it to the blues and rhythm-and-blues coming over the radio, set the piano-accordion at its centre, and had the modern frottoir bent into shape by a welder. It is close kin to Cajun music but distinct: Creole rather than white Acadian, bluesier and more syncopated, built to drive a hard two-step or waltz all night.

The name

“Zydeco” is a Louisiana Creole word, worn down from les haricots in the phrase “les haricots sont pas salés” — “the snap-beans aren't salted”, a joke about being too poor to afford the salt-meat that seasons the pot. This machine keeps the phrase for its title, Pas Salé, and names itself in French, the mother tongue of the music.
