on this music

In 1968 the Hungarian-Dutch biologist Aristid Lindenmayer proposed a formal grammar for the growth of filamentous algae: a string of symbols where every symbol is rewritten at once, every step, by fixed rules. An L-system is just an axiom and a set of parallel productions — A → AB, B → A — and yet the strings that grow out of them carry the same branching self-similarity you see in ferns, willows and romanesco. Prusinkiewicz and Lindenmayer's The Algorithmic Beauty of Plants (1990) showed that reading the string as turtle commands — forward, turn, branch — draws the plant itself.

GERMEN performs the string. The grammar is expanded exactly, with no randomness: the same species at the same depth always grows the same string, which you can verify by hand. Then a second turtle walks it as a score: every F strikes a plucked string, + and − raise and lower the scale degree, and brackets [ ] push and pop the state — so a side-branch is a little melody that leaves the main stem untouched, higher and quicker the deeper it nests, exactly as a real branch leaves the trunk where it found it. The drawing and the melody come from the same walk: what you see light up is the note you hear.

The result sounds like music because self-similarity is the oldest compositional trick there is — a motive returning at every scale, a theme inside a theme. The seed is the season, not the species: it decides the weather (small ornaments, grace-note doublings, the reverb's stone) but never touches the grammar. Change the law — species, depth, mode — and a new plant grows at the next cycle.

Everything is synthesized: the plucks are Karplus–Strong strings, the drone is two oscillators, the room is a seeded noise impulse. No samples, no network, one file.
