On this music · the Javanese gamelan

What you're hearing

A gamelan is an orchestra of tuned bronze — hanging gongs, kettle gongs on racks, thick keyed metallophones — built and tuned as one instrument, played by a community as one body. This is the Central Javanese court tradition, the karawitan of Surakarta and Yogyakarta: music that does not march from a beginning to an end but turns in circles, each circle closed by the deepest sound in the ensemble, the great gong. What you hear is one small gendhing — two cycles, composed by the machine within the rules below, elaborated the way a gamelan elaborates.

The cycle is the law

Javanese time is colotomic: instruments of different sizes divide the cycle at different depths, and where they fall is fixed by the bentuk, the form — it is the form. The little kethuk ticks the smallest marks; the kenong, brightest and longest-ringing of the kettles, quarters the cycle; the hanging kempul answers between the kenong strokes (its first slot is left empty — the wela, a held breath); and the gong ageng, the great gong, sounds once, at the end, which is also the beginning. The rarer the stroke, the deeper the instrument. Lancaran packs its punctuation close, sixteen brisk beats; ketawang opens the same sixteen into something songlike; ladrang stretches to thirty-two, stately. Pick the form and you have picked the skeleton of time itself; nothing else is allowed to move those strokes.

Laras — no two gamelans agree

Gamelan music uses two tunings, and neither is the Western scale. Sléndro divides the octave into five roughly even steps — wide seconds, no semitones, a scale that floats. Pélog is seven unequal tones from which five are drawn at a time — narrow steps crowding against wide ones, dark and perfumed. And crucially: there is no standard pitch and no standard temperament. Every gamelan is tuned to itself — its own embat, the tuner's personal curve — and instruments are meaningless outside their own set. The tuning here is this machine's own embat, one fixed voice inside the documented ranges of real gamelans; a neighbouring gamelan would disagree with it, and that disagreement is the tradition working as intended.

Pathet — the mode

Within a laras, a pathet constrains the melody the way a mode does: some tones are homes to cadence on (sèlèh), some are passing colour, one or two are all but avoided. Sléndro carries three — nem, grave and even, for the evening's first watch of the shadow play; sanga, inward, for the deep middle of the night; manyura, bright and lifting, for the hours before dawn. Pélog answers with lima, nem and barang — the last swapping tone 1 out for the high glint of tone 7. The machine composes its skeleton by walking toward sèlèh tones the pathet approves, and every cycle ends on the pathet's own gong tone.

Irama — the elastic clock

Gamelan tempo is not one number but a ratio. As the beat of the skeleton slows, the elaborating instruments double their notes to fill the widening space — so the music gets slower and busier at once, like a wheel turning down through gears. These levels are the irama. Here tanggung puts two elaborating notes to each beat, dadi four, wilet eight; the laya slider sets the walking pace the levels divide. At wilet a sixteen-beat cycle can take most of a minute, the peking raining sixteenth-notes the whole way.

Balungan and garap

Gamelan musicians do not read parts. There is one skeleton melody — the balungan, "the bones," one tone to a beat, shown here in the cipher notation (kepatihan) Javanese musicians actually use — and every instrument derives its part from those bones by the rules of its role. That derivation is called garap, the working. The demung and saron strike the bones plainly. The little peking doubles each pair of tones in its clockwork way. The bonang barung walks ahead of the saron, sounding each coming pair before it arrives — the pattern called pipilan — and dropping to ringing octaves (gembyang) to announce a cadence; the bonang panerus does the same at twice the speed. The kendhang, the only drum, holds the ensemble's reins. Change the balungan and every part changes with it: that is the whole architecture.

The bronze

Bronze does not ring in neat harmonics; its partials land between the cracks of the harmonic series, which is why a gamelan shimmers. The great gong is tuned — deliberately — so that two of its vibrating modes sit a few cycles apart: the slow throb you hear in its ring is the ombak, the wave, beating at the difference. The saron player's left hand damps each key just as the right strikes the next, so the line stays clean; listen for the dry edge of each bone as the next one lands. Both are rendered here — the beat rate of the gong and the damping hand alike.

A note on faithfulness

The forms, the colotomy, the pathet cadence habits, the nacah and pipilan figuration and the irama ratios are the documented grammar of Central Javanese practice. The balungan themselves are composed by the machine — idiomatic, not quotations — and the drumming is a simplified kendhang kalih outline. A real karawitan carries far more than this: the rebab and the voices, gendér and gambang floating free of the grid, the whole garment of which this is the loom. What is here is the loom, honestly threaded.

How the sound is made

No samples. Every instrument is additive synthesis from first principles: the keyed bars are a fundamental with the high inharmonic partials of a free metal bar, struck in slightly detuned pairs; the kettle gongs add the hum and bend of a domed boss; the great gong is two close-tuned modes beating at the ombak rate under a slow bloom; the kendhang is a struck membrane. The whole gendhing — two gongan — is rendered once into a stereo buffer, given a breath of pavilion air, and looped, the gong's long ring folded across the seam so the circle truly closes. The WAV you cut is exactly what you hear.

The name

op. XIV is named in Javanese, the mother tongue of this music: a gongan is the span from one stroke of the great gong to the next — the breath-length of Javanese time, the circle everything here lives inside.
