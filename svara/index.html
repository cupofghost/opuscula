On this music · Carnatic svara and shruti

What you're hearing

South Indian Carnatic music: a raga performed as a short concert item — a free-rhythm ālāpana exploring the raga over a droning tambura, a pallavi line elaborated through repeated sangati variations against a mridangam, and a closing korvai, a phrase stated three times that must land exactly on the downbeat.

Śruti — the twenty-two

Indian music theory since Bharata's Nāṭyaśāstra (~200 BCE–200 CE) counts twenty-two shrutis to the octave — not an equal division, but the exact just-intonation positions melody actually visits. Ten of the twelve semitone regions split into a pair of shrutis a syntonic comma (81/80, about a fifth of a semitone) apart; Sa and Pa never split. A raga's choice of which shruti in a pair to sing is not decoration — it is part of what makes the raga itself. Kharaharapriyā's ri and Śaṅkarābharaṇaṁ's ri are "the same" note, a comma apart.

Gamaka — the swara's own identity

A swara is not a point but an ornamented motion. In many ragas certain notes are never sung plain — the oscillation, slide, or grace note is the note's identity, not something added to it. Every turning point of every ornament here is itself an exact shruti.

Rāga as law

A raga is not just a scale: it is an ascending and a descending path (which may omit notes, or differ from each other), resting tones, notes the melody dwells on, and characteristic phrases. The eight ragas here carry their own such rules, and the melody-generator is only ever allowed to move within them.

The korvai

A korvai closes the item: a phrase stated three times with equal gaps between, sized so precisely that the final stroke falls exactly on samam, the downbeat of the rhythmic cycle. It is pure arithmetic with one right answer.

The name

Svara — from svayaṁ rājate, "that which shines of itself" — is Sanskrit for the musical note itself.
