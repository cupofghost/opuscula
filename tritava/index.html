On this music · the scale with no octave

What you're hearing

A generative piece composed entirely inside the Bohlen–Pierce scale — a tuning that abandons the octave. A soft reed drone holds the ground; glass bells arpeggiate a progression of Bohlen–Pierce chords; a clarinet-voiced line threads a melody through the chosen mode; a low tick keeps the meter. Every pitch is an exact whole-number ratio; nothing here is equal-tempered, and — this is the strange part — no two notes anywhere are an octave apart on purpose. The loop is composed once from the seed and played seamlessly; the WAV you cut is exactly what you hear.

The octave, and then not the octave

Double any frequency and the ear calls it "the same note, higher" — that is the octave, the ratio 2:1, and nearly every scale ever built repeats itself at it. Bohlen and Pierce asked a heretical question: what if a scale repeated at the tritave instead — the ratio 3:1, an octave-plus-a-fifth, what musicians call a perfect twelfth? Divide that wider space into thirteen steps and you get a complete harmonic language that is genuinely alien and genuinely consonant — but one in which the octave, the most basic interval in all other music, simply does not occur.

Where it came from

Heinz Bohlen, a German microwave-electronics engineer and amateur trumpeter, derived the scale around 1972 from the way combination tones behave, taking as his law that a chord sounds consonant when its tones fall in the ratio 3:5:7. Kees van Prooijen reached the same scale independently a few years later. Then John R. Pierce — the Bell Labs engineer who named the transistor and launched the first communications satellite — rediscovered it in the 1980s with Max Mathews, the father of computer music. Their crucial contribution was acoustic, not mathematical: using synthesized tones, they showed that the scale's chords only lock into consonance when the timbre is built from odd harmonics — the spectrum of a clarinet, or a pipe stopped at one end. On ordinary bright, even-harmonic tones the same chords sound sour.

Why everything here is a reed or a stopped pipe

That acoustic fact is this machine's design. Every pitched voice — the drone, the bells, the melody, the tick — is synthesized from odd harmonics only (1, 3, 5, 7, 9…), the square-ish, hollow, woody spectrum in which Bohlen–Pierce harmony actually rings true. The tuning dictates the timbre; you are not hearing a synth pad decorated with an exotic scale, you are hearing the only kind of tone in which this scale means anything.

The just scale — thirteen steps, no tempering

There is an equal-tempered Bohlen–Pierce (thirteen equal divisions of the tritave, each the thirteenth root of 3). This machine does not use it. It uses the just Bohlen–Pierce scale: thirteen exact ratios whose only prime factors are 3, 5 and 7 — 1/1, 27/25, 25/21, 9/7, 7/5, 75/49, 5/3, 9/5, 49/25, 15/7, 7/3, 63/25, 25/9, and home again at 3/1. Where every other machine in this collection that touches tuning insists on exact ratios, so does this one: there is no equal temperament anywhere in the signal path.

The modes

Lambda is the scale's home mode — a nine-note diatonic subset (a "whole tone" of 25/21 to open, a lead-tone semitone to close, never two whole tones in a row) and the mode in which the scale was first derived; it is the friendliest doorway in. Gamma is another of its diatonic modes, two five-note runs bridged by a step, more restless. Harmonia is a six-note distillation to the very simplest consonances the scale owns — 1/1, 9/7, 7/5, 5/3, 9/5, 7/3. And Chromatic opens all thirteen steps at once, the full alien gamut.

The chords

Bohlen–Pierce's answer to the major chord is the ratio 3:5:7 — three tones drawn straight from the odd overtones, bright and outward, the sonority the whole scale was reasoned backward from. Its cooler shade here is 5:7:9. The machine walks a seeded progression of these chords over the degrees of the chosen mode, resolving home to the 3:5:7 on the tonic at every phrase. The wheel on the plate draws each chord as a triangle stretched across the thirteen spokes; because the spokes are spaced by their true ratios and the ring closes at the tritave, the whole geometry is a picture of a scale that never returns to an octave.

The name

op. XIX is named in Latin, the collection's tongue for tuning theory: tritava — the tri- of "three" fused to the -tave of "octave," the coined English word tritave Latinised. It names the one interval this music repeats at, and quietly, the one interval it repeats at instead.
