On this music · a machine that predicts itself

What you're hearing

A small mind waking up. It begins knowing nothing, and it composes the only way a language model can: it looks at what it has played so far, forms a guess about what should come next, picks one note from that guess, and lets the note become part of what it looks at. Over and over. As it plays it learns its own habits — and, because it can only remember so far back, it also forgets them, and is surprised by its own past when they return.

Expectation is the shared channel

Music theory has long held that feeling in music is expectation and its outcome. Leonard Meyer argued it in 1956; David Huron's ITPRA theory built the psychology of it; a line of research (IDyOM) showed that a simple statistical next-note model's surprise predicts the tension a listener feels, note for note. Tension is high surprise; resolution is low surprise; a style is just a learned set of expectations. That is also, exactly, what a next-token predictor is. So the one aesthetic channel a machine like this and a human ear genuinely share is expectation — and here it is rendered honestly: what you see and hear is the model's real surprise, computed as −log₂ of the probability it gave the note it chose.

The voices

The lead voice sings each chosen note; the more surprised the model is, the brighter and sharper it cuts, while a confident note is soft and round. Under it, a faint glassy halo sounds the notes that could have been — the other candidates, each as loud as the model thought it likely. When the model is certain, the halo falls silent: the guess has already collapsed into one note. A just-intonation drone holds the ground the surprise is felt against, and a barely-there pulse fidgets when the mind is uncertain and goes still when it settles.

The name

Latin tessera — a token: the tile of a mosaic, the broken token of hospitality, and the soldier's daily watchword. All three fit. The piece is a mosaic laid one tile at a time; each tile is drawn from a distribution; and the mosaic so far is the context the next tile is drawn from.

An honest note on the frame

This machine was designed by a large language model that was asked what kind of machine it would like, if it could experience one. This is the loop it lives in, made small enough to be exact and to hear. The model that composes each pressing is not a portrait of the designer — it is the same kind of thing, the smallest one that is still really it.
